this is one reason so much digital life feels strangely joyless even when it is functioning.


the friction does not always announce itself as a problem. often it presents itself as normal adulthood. of course you should have a better system. of course you should organize your accounts. of course you should clean up your notifications, reset your permissions, review your backups, adjust your settings, update your payment method, check the family plan, reauthenticate the device, make a smarter routine.

some of that is fair.

but some of it is just work that used to belong to the product.

that is the part i keep circling.



i've become tolerant of needless complexity.


it makes us more managerial in our private lives. more  more willing to accept that every convenience must be paid for with small recurring acts of attention. it trains us to treat unfinished systems as normal and to experience the resulting burden as a personal organization problem.

no wonder finished things feel almost moral by comparison.

a good pan does not need to know who you are. a decent bookshelf does not require a decision tree. a zipper does not introduce itself. a kitchen timer does not ask for permission to notify your household. the best umbrella i own does one thing with such lack of drama that i am grateful for it every time it rains.





these objects are not virtuous because they are old-fashioned. they are virtuous because they stay in bounds.

they do not keep expanding the definition of the relationship.

that boundedness creates a very particular kind of relief.

not silence, exactly. more like the absence of follow-up.

you use the thing and the thing lets you go.

it does not leave a residue of admin behind it. it does not convert a mundane task into a chain of secondary tasks. it does not make you feel that ownership is a role you now need to perform.

that should not feel luxurious, but now it does.

i do not want a life with fewer tools. i want a life with more tools that know when they are done.



I keep coming back to the same thought: a lot of technology is not badly intentioned so much as badly mannered.

I have started using a very simple test for software: after a week with it, do I feel helped, or do I feel subtly in trouble?

Not “frustrated” in the obvious sense. Something more specific than that.

Accused.

The accusation can take a lot of forms.

Sometimes it sounds like productivity. You open an app and it immediately tells you what is pending, overdue, unreviewed, unseen, unfinished. The screen is technically informative, but the emotional tone is already set. You are late to your own tools.

Sometimes it sounds like self-improvement. You have not checked in. You broke the streak. You missed the recap. You still have items to process. You left value on the table.

Sometimes it sounds like housekeeping. Your storage is nearly full. Your plan needs updating. Your device needs attention. Your session expired. Your settings changed. Your account requires action.

Again, none of these prompts are individually absurd. The problem is their cumulative voice.

Software keeps finding ways to cast the user as the lagging party.

I do not think that is accidental.

A lot of interface design now is built around recurrence. The system wants you back in the app. It wants you back on the page. It wants a durable behavioral loop. So every surface is tuned to preserve salience. Something is always waiting. Something is always almost slipping. Something is always asking to be cleared, restored, caught up, or completed.

That constant state of low-grade summons changes how a product feels.

It stops feeling like equipment and starts feeling like a supervisor.

I notice this most in places that should feel generous.

Reading software is a good example. I still think feeds are one of the great ideas on the web. But a feed stops being a feed the moment it starts acting like a debt ledger. If every unread item becomes a mark against you, the whole thing curdles. Curiosity turns into backlog management.

That is a design choice, not a law of nature.

There are much better metaphors available. Streams. Rivers. Shelves. Tables. Places where things can wait without pressing charges.

The same problem shows up in learning tools.

I like audio for the same reason most people do: it fits into the scraps of the day. Walking, commuting, washing dishes, carrying groceries. It makes room for ideas where a screen cannot.

But a lot of audio software confuses access with learning. It is very good at moving material past your ears. It is less good at helping any of it remain. You can finish something, feel stimulated, and still be unable to recover the actual argument two days later.

That failure is usually blamed on the listener.

You should have paid better attention. You should have taken notes. You should have been more disciplined.

Maybe. But the interface also matters. If the medium gives you no landmarks, no built-in summaries, no obvious return points, no place for your own thinking to attach, then of course the experience becomes vaporous. The system provided exposure and called it understanding.

This matters because it is the same move again.

The tool creates the condition, then frames the consequence as your shortcoming.

That is also what happens with support.

When a big platform makes account recovery impossible, or hides actual help behind loops of canned forms, the repair work does not disappear. It just moves.

It lands on the sibling who “gets computers.” The friend who is patient with settings. The adult child who gets the text when a login goes sideways. Entire companies quietly rely on the fact that someone adjacent to the real user will absorb the mess for free.

That is not customer support. That is outsourced care work with no name and no budget.

It is one reason I have become suspicious of any story that reduces our technological exhaustion to personal discipline.

Discipline matters.

So do incentives, defaults, metaphors, product priorities, support budgets, and the basic willingness of a company to let a tool be merely useful instead of behaviorally sticky.

Good software has a different posture.

It does not rush to the center of your attention unless something genuinely requires it.

It does not turn abundance into guilt.

It does not make ordinary use feel like you are slowly failing an invisible class.

It does not assume that the user exists to stabilize the weaknesses of the system.

And when it helps you learn something, it gives the thought somewhere to rest.

That last point matters more to me than it used to.

The older I get, the less impressed I am by systems that feel engaging in the moment but leave no durable trace. I am not looking for more stimulation. I am looking for tools that help me keep what mattered.

The same is true more broadly.

I want software that helps without hovering.

I want interfaces that present possibility without moralizing it.

I want products that fail plainly when they fail, and that do not conscript users and their families into patching over the result.

I want tools that can remain in the periphery until invited.

Mostly, I want software that does not keep trying to make me feel at fault for the shape of the environment it created.

Tools should be capable, legible, and a little bit humble.

They should not need this much theater.

They should not need this much psychology.

They should not accuse us so often.
